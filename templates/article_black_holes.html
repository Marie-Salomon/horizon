Black Holes: Beyond the Event Horizon

Classification: TOP SECRET / LEVEL 4 ACCESS

The Gravity Trap

A black hole is an object so massive and compact that it prevents even light from escaping its grasp. To understand why, we look at Escape Velocity.

Note: Contrary to popular belief, black holes are not "giant vacuum cleaners." They only swallow matter and radiation that stray too close to their influence.

Born from Dead Stars

A black hole is born when a massive star dies. Following a colossal explosion (Supernova), the outer layers are ejected while the core collapses under its own gravity. What remains is a core so dense it warps the very fabric of space-time.

The Three Classes of Monsters

1. Stellar Black Holes

The most common type. They are the remains of massive stars, typically weighing 10 to 20 times the mass of our Sun. The "lightest" known stellar black hole is 3.8 solar masses, which is still over 7 x 10¬≥‚Å∞ kg!

2. Supermassive Black Holes

The heavyweights. They weigh millions or billions of times our Sun's mass. They hide at the center of most galaxies, like Sagittarius A* at the heart of our Milky Way. Their origin remains a mystery, likely forming from the fusion of many smaller black holes.

3. Primordial Black Holes (Theoretical)

Hypothetical tiny black holes born shortly after the Big Bang. Imagine the mass of an entire planet condensed into a marble just a few millimeters wide. They have never been detected, but their existence remains a key scientific theory.

Evidence & Observation

For decades, black holes were only theoretical. This changed in April 2019, when the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) released the first direct image of the M87* accretion disk.
