As the universe cooled, electrons slowed down enough to be captured by atomic nuclei, forming the very first atoms of hydrogen and helium. This was the birth of matter as we know it.

Until then, photons (the particles of light) were trapped in a dense, opaque "cosmic soup." Suddenly, they were free to travel through space: the Universe lit up in a magnificent flash.

However, this brilliance was short-lived. Since stars and galaxies had not yet formed, the Universe eventually faded into what scientists call the "Dark Ages," waiting millions of years for the first stars to ignite.

The Echo of Creation: Cosmic Microwave Background

The most definitive proof of our origins is the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB). This is the "first light" of the Universeâ€”the monumental flash that occurred 380,000 years after the Big Bang.

Theorized in the late 1940s, this "fossil radiation" was actually detected by accident in 1965. Two American physicists, while testing a ultra-sensitive radio antenna, picked up a mysterious background noise they couldn't explain. This "hiss" turned out to be the afterglow of the Big Bang, earning them the Nobel Prize in Physics.

Today, this light still exists as a uniform hum of radio waves present everywhere in the cosmos. Between 2009 and 2013, the European Planck satellite successfully "photographed" this radiation with unprecedented precision, providing us with the oldest map of our Universe.
